Part 1The big picture

We want a computer to look at a microscope image of a dog's skin and automatically find the cancer (melanoma) regions — the same job a veterinary pathologist does by eye.

Simple analogy 🧠 — Think of teaching a child with flashcards. First you prepare clean, consistent flashcards (Weeks 1–2). Then you show the child thousands of examples until it learns to point at the tumour (Weeks 3–4). That's exactly the two halves of this project.
🎬 Say this in the video: "Dog skin cancer looks a lot like human non-UV melanoma. There's a public dataset of 750 expert-labelled dog tumour slides called CATCH. Our goal is to train an AI to segment — to outline — the tumour regions automatically."

Step 2 · Quality check

The computer measures each slide's sharpness, brightness and how much actual tissue it contains. Bad slides are flagged and excluded with a written reason — so we only train on good data.

🎬 Say: "Garbage in, garbage out — so we automatically reject blurry or empty slides first."
Step 3 · Stain normalisation (Macenko)

Different labs and scanners make the same tissue look pink, purple or blue. The Macenko method re-colours every slide to one standard look, so the AI learns shapes and patterns — not the colour of a particular scanner.

🎬 Say: "This is like applying the same colour filter to every photo so lighting differences don't confuse the model."
Step 4 · Patch extraction (multi-magnification)

The slide is too big for any AI, so we cut it into small 256×256 squares. We do this at three zoom levels (5×, 10×, 20×) so the model sees both the fine detail and the big picture. Empty/background tiles are dropped.

🎬 Say: "We tile the slide like a chessboard and keep only the squares that actually contain tissue — at three different zoom levels."
Step 5 · Stratified split

The patches are divided into three groups: train (to learn from), validation (to tune), and test (a final unseen exam). The split is at the slide level so no slide leaks between groups — that would be cheating.

  • Result of Weeks 1–2: a clean, colour-consistent, tiled dataset ready for training.
  • One command to reproduce it: python scripts\run_week1_2_pipeline.py --demo

Step 2 · Data augmentation

We randomly flip, rotate and colour-jitter the training patches. This shows the model many variations of each example so it generalises instead of memorising.

Step 3 · The U-Net — two versions

Following the project plan, we train two models to compare:

① Baseline U-Net

Built from scratch, learns everything from zero. Our reference point.

② U-Net + ResNet-34

Starts from a network already trained on millions of images (ImageNet) — "transfer learning". Should learn faster and better.

🎬 Say: "We compare learning from scratch versus starting from a model that already knows what edges and textures look like."
Step 4 · How learning works

For each patch the model guesses a mask, we measure how wrong it is with a loss (a mix of "BCE" and "Dice"), and the model nudges itself to do better. We repeat this for many epochs (passes over the data). We watch the loss go down and the accuracy go up live in TensorBoard.

🎬 Say: "Every round, the model gets a score for how close its outline is to the real one, and corrects itself. We literally watch it get smarter on a graph."
Step 5 · Evaluation metrics

On unseen test patches we measure overlap between predicted and true tumour:

  • Dice score — the headline metric (1.0 = perfect overlap).
  • IoU — another overlap measure.
  • Pixel accuracy & Hausdorff distance — supporting checks.
🎬 Say: "Dice is just: how much does my predicted outline overlap the real one? Higher is better."

These numbers are from synthetic test data that proves the whole pipeline works; the real CATCH slides come next."
Important honesty note 📌 — these scores are from synthetic (computer-made) slides used to prove the machinery runs end-to-end while the real dataset download is finalised. The exact numbers will change on real data; the proposal target is Dice > 0.85.
