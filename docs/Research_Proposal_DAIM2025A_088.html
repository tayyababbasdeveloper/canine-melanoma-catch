Section A Aims and Objectives

The principal aim of this research is to develop and evaluate deep learning-based approaches for the automated classification of malignant melanoma in canine cutaneous histopathology images, utilising the CAnine CuTaneous Cancer Histology (CATCH) dataset (Fragoso-Garcia et al., 2023). This work seeks to contribute toward the broader understanding of melanoma pathology across species, given the well-documented histological parallels between canine and human melanomas (Gillard et al., 2014).

Specific Objectives

1
To perform semantic segmentation of canine cutaneous tumour regions within whole-slide histopathology images using a U-Net architecture with transfer learning.
2
To develop and train convolutional neural network (CNN) classifiers, including ResNet-50 and EfficientNet-B3, for the multi-class classification of canine cutaneous tumour subtypes.
3
To evaluate model performance through rigorous quantitative metrics (accuracy, F1-score, precision, recall, and AUC-ROC) and qualitative assessment of segmentation outputs.
4
To analyse the clinical relevance of classification outcomes in the context of comparative oncology, examining whether findings may inform non-UV-induced melanoma research in humans.
Section B Background and Literature Review

2.1 Malignant Melanoma: Clinical Context

Malignant melanoma is a highly aggressive neoplasm arising from melanocytes, pigment-producing cells found across diverse anatomical regions. In humans, melanoma accounts for approximately 5% of all skin cancers, yet it is responsible for a disproportionate 75% of skin cancer-related mortality (Schadendorf et al., 2018). The major subtypes include cutaneous melanoma (both UV-induced and non-UV-induced), acral melanoma, mucosal melanoma, and ocular melanoma. Each subtype demonstrates distinct epidemiological patterns, histopathological features, genetic profiles, and prognostic outcomes (Shain and Bastian, 2016).

Cutaneous melanoma predominates in fair-skinned populations and is strongly associated with ultraviolet (UV) radiation exposure. However, non-UV-induced melanomas—including mucosal and acral subtypes—present a distinct molecular landscape characterised by different driver mutations, notably in the KIT and NRAS oncogenes rather than the BRAF mutations commonly observed in UV-associated melanomas (Curtin et al., 2005). Understanding these non-UV pathways is paramount for developing targeted therapeutic strategies.

2.2 Canine Melanoma as a Comparative Oncology Model

Canine malignant melanomas share substantial histological and molecular similarities with their human counterparts, particularly the non-UV-induced variants (Gillard et al., 2014). Oral melanoma, the most prevalent form in dogs, occurs naturally without UV radiation involvement, offering a valuable translational model for investigating non-UV melanoma biology. Certain breeds—including Scottish Terriers, Golden Retrievers, Poodles, and Cocker Spaniels—exhibit a heightened genetic predisposition, suggesting heritable risk factors that may parallel human familial melanoma susceptibility (Nishiya et al., 2016).

The comparative oncology framework posits that studying spontaneously occurring cancers in companion animals can yield insights directly transferable to human medicine. Canine tumours progress through similar stages, share equivalent histological grades, and respond to analogous treatment modalities, making them an ethically advantageous and scientifically rigorous model for preclinical investigation (Prouteau and André, 2019).

2.3 The CATCH Dataset

The CAnine CuTaneous Cancer Histology (CATCH) dataset, curated by Fragoso-Garcia et al. (2023), comprises 750 whole-slide images of canine cutaneous tumours, representing a diverse array of histological subtypes. The dataset was developed to facilitate computational pathology research and includes expert-annotated tumour region delineations. Available through The Cancer Imaging Archive (Clark et al., 2013), the CATCH dataset provides a standardised benchmark for the development and validation of segmentation and classification algorithms.

Each sample within the dataset has been meticulously reviewed by board-certified veterinary pathologists, ensuring diagnostic accuracy. The diversity of tumour subtypes represented—including melanocytic tumours, mast cell tumours, squamous cell carcinomas, and others—offers a challenging multi-class classification problem that mirrors the complexity encountered in clinical diagnostic workflows.

2.4 Deep Learning in Histopathology Image Analysis

The application of deep learning to histopathological image analysis has demonstrated transformative potential across multiple cancer types. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have achieved diagnostic performance comparable to, and in some instances surpassing, that of expert pathologists (Esteva et al., 2017). Architectures such as ResNet (He et al., 2016), VGG (Simonyan and Zisserman, 2015), and EfficientNet (Tan and Le, 2019) have been extensively employed for classification tasks, leveraging transfer learning from large-scale natural image datasets such as ImageNet to overcome the limited sample sizes common in medical imaging.

For semantic segmentation—the pixel-level delineation of tumour regions—the U-Net architecture has emerged as a standard framework in biomedical image analysis (Ronneberger et al., 2015). Its encoder-decoder structure with skip connections enables precise localisation while maintaining contextual understanding. Variants such as Attention U-Net (Oktay et al., 2018) have further enhanced segmentation performance by incorporating attention mechanisms that selectively emphasise relevant spatial features.

Recent studies have applied these architectures specifically to dermatopathology, with promising results. Hekler et al. (2019) demonstrated that a CNN-based classifier could distinguish melanoma from nevi in human histopathology images with accuracy exceeding 90%. Similarly, Brinker et al. (2019) showed that ensemble approaches combining multiple CNN architectures yielded robust classification performance across heterogeneous datasets. However, relatively few studies have applied such methods to veterinary histopathology, representing a notable research gap that this project intends to address.

2.5 Addressing the Research Gap

Despite the established parallels between canine and human melanomas, computational approaches to canine cutaneous tumour classification remain markedly underexplored. The availability of the CATCH dataset presents a timely opportunity to apply state-of-the-art deep learning methods to this domain. By developing accurate segmentation and classification models for canine cutaneous tumours, this research may contribute to both veterinary diagnostic practice and the broader comparative oncology agenda, potentially illuminating pathways relevant to non-UV melanoma in humans.

Section C Research Description and Anticipated Outcomes

3.1 Proposed Methodology

The proposed research follows a structured pipeline encompassing data acquisition and preprocessing, model development for both segmentation and classification tasks, systematic evaluation, and comparative analysis. Each phase has been designed to align with established best practices in computational pathology while addressing the specific characteristics of the CATCH dataset.

Figure 2: Sequential stages of the proposed research methodology, from data acquisition through to comparative analysis.

3.1.1 Data Acquisition and Preprocessing

The CATCH dataset will be obtained from The Cancer Imaging Archive (TCIA). Preprocessing will involve standardising image dimensions, applying stain normalisation using the Macenko method (Macenko et al., 2009) to mitigate inter-slide colour variability, and performing patch extraction at multiple magnification levels (5×, 10×, 20×). Data augmentation strategies—including rotation, horizontal and vertical flipping, elastic deformation, and colour jittering—will be employed to enhance model generalisation and address class imbalance. The dataset will be partitioned into training (70%), validation (15%), and test (15%) subsets using stratified sampling to preserve class distributions.

3.1.2 Segmentation Pipeline

Tumour region segmentation will be performed using a U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015) with a ResNet-34 encoder pretrained on ImageNet. The encoder will extract hierarchical feature representations, while the decoder will reconstruct spatial resolution through transposed convolutions and skip connections. An Attention U-Net variant (Oktay et al., 2018) will also be implemented to assess the impact of attention gating on segmentation precision. Both models will be trained using a composite loss function combining binary cross-entropy and Dice loss, optimised with the Adam optimiser at an initial learning rate of 1×10-4 with cosine annealing scheduling.

3.1.3 Classification Pipeline

Two classification architectures will be evaluated: ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) and EfficientNet-B3 (Tan and Le, 2019). Both models will be initialised with ImageNet-pretrained weights and fine-tuned on the CATCH dataset. A progressive unfreezing strategy will be adopted, initially training only the classification head before gradually unfreezing deeper convolutional layers. The models will be trained using cross-entropy loss with label smoothing (factor = 0.1) and optimised using AdamW with weight decay regularisation. Hyperparameter tuning will be conducted through systematic grid search over learning rates, batch sizes, and dropout rates.

3.1.4 Evaluation Framework

Segmentation performance will be assessed using the Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC), Intersection over Union (IoU), pixel-wise accuracy, and Hausdorff distance. Classification performance will be evaluated through accuracy, macro-averaged precision, recall, F1-score, and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC). Confusion matrices will provide granular insights into per-class performance, while Grad-CAM visualisations (Selvaraju et al., 2017) will offer interpretability by highlighting the image regions most influential to classification decisions. Five-fold cross-validation will ensure robust and generalisable performance estimates.

3.2 Anticipated Outcomes

Based upon the reviewed literature and the characteristics of the CATCH dataset, the following outcomes are anticipated:

Outcome 1: Segmentation Accuracy
The U-Net models are expected to achieve a Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) exceeding 0.85 for tumour region delineation, consistent with performance benchmarks reported in comparable histopathology segmentation studies (Ronneberger et al., 2015). The Attention U-Net variant is anticipated to yield modest improvements, particularly for smaller or irregularly shaped tumour regions.
Outcome 2: Classification Performance
Transfer learning-based classifiers are expected to achieve multi-class classification accuracy of 85–92%, with EfficientNet-B3 anticipated to demonstrate superior efficiency owing to its compound scaling strategy. Melanocytic tumours specifically are expected to be classified with an F1-score exceeding 0.88, given their distinct histological features.
Outcome 3: Model Interpretability
Grad-CAM visualisations are expected to reveal that the classification models attend to histologically meaningful features—such as melanin pigmentation patterns, nuclear morphology, and tissue architecture—thereby validating the clinical relevance of the learned representations.
Outcome 4: Comparative Oncology Insights
The classification patterns observed may illuminate morphological features shared between canine cutaneous melanomas and human non-UV-induced melanomas, providing preliminary computational evidence for cross-species translational pathology research.

3.3 Justification for the Research Approach

The selection of U-Net for segmentation is grounded in its established efficacy in biomedical imaging contexts, where its skip-connection architecture demonstrates particular strength in preserving fine-grained spatial detail essential for accurate tumour delineation (Ronneberger et al., 2015). The inclusion of Attention U-Net provides a controlled comparison to evaluate whether attention mechanisms yield statistically significant improvements in the specific context of canine cutaneous histopathology.

ResNet-50 was selected for its proven track record in medical image classification and its residual learning framework, which mitigates the vanishing gradient problem in deep networks (He et al., 2016). EfficientNet-B3 was chosen as a complementary architecture due to its superior parameter efficiency, achieved through compound scaling of network width, depth, and resolution (Tan and Le, 2019). Evaluating both architectures enables a rigorous comparison of classification approaches with differing computational profiles.

Transfer learning is justified by the relatively modest size of the CATCH dataset (750 samples). Fine-tuning ImageNet-pretrained models has been shown to substantially outperform training from scratch in medical imaging settings with limited data availability (Esteva et al., 2017). The progressive unfreezing strategy further safeguards against catastrophic forgetting of learned feature representations.

4.2 Task Breakdown and Discussion

Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1–3)

The initial phase establishes the research foundation. During Week 1, the computational environment will be configured, including the installation of PyTorch, CUDA-enabled GPU drivers, and auxiliary libraries (OpenSlide, Albumentations, scikit-learn). Concurrently, the literature review will be refined to incorporate the most recent publications. Weeks 1–3 will encompass the complete data acquisition and preprocessing pipeline: downloading the CATCH dataset from TCIA, performing quality assessment of whole-slide images, applying Macenko stain normalisation, and extracting annotated patches at multiple resolutions. This phase is critical, as the quality of preprocessing directly impacts all subsequent model performance. Approximately 40 hours of computational time have been allocated for patch extraction from 750 whole-slide images.

Phase 2: Model Development (Weeks 3–8)

The development phase represents the core of the research programme. Weeks 3–5 will focus on implementing and training the U-Net segmentation model, beginning with baseline architecture before incorporating the ResNet-34 encoder. Hyperparameter optimisation will proceed iteratively, with training monitored via TensorBoard for loss convergence and validation metrics. The Attention U-Net variant will be implemented during Weeks 5–6. Classification model development commences in Week 6 with ResNet-50, followed by EfficientNet-B3 in Weeks 7–8. Each model will require approximately 8–12 hours of GPU training time per epoch configuration, justifying the two-week allocation per architecture. Progressive unfreezing schedules will be documented meticulously for reproducibility.

Phase 3: Evaluation and Analysis (Weeks 8–10)

Weeks 8–9 will be dedicated to comprehensive model evaluation through five-fold cross-validation, computation of performance metrics, generation of confusion matrices, and statistical significance testing (McNemar's test) to compare architectures. Grad-CAM visualisations will be produced during Week 9 to assess model interpretability. Weeks 9–10 will focus on results analysis, contextualising findings within the comparative oncology literature and drawing connections between observed classification patterns and established histopathological features.

Phase 4: Documentation and Submission (Weeks 8–12)

Dissertation writing will commence during Week 8, running in parallel with the evaluation phase to maintain momentum. The introduction, background, and methodology chapters will be drafted first, followed by results and discussion sections as experimental outcomes are finalised. Weeks 11–12 are reserved for comprehensive revision, supervisor feedback incorporation, proofreading, and formatting. Week 12 includes a dedicated contingency buffer to accommodate any unforeseen delays.

4.3 Risk Assessment and Contingency Planning

A comprehensive risk assessment has been conducted to identify potential impediments to the project timeline and to establish appropriate mitigation strategies. The following table summarises the key risks, their assessed likelihood and impact, and the proposed contingency measures.

Risk Likelihood Impact Contingency Strategy
Insufficient GPU Resources Medium High Google Colab Pro and university HPC cluster have been identified as alternative compute resources. Model architectures can be scaled down (e.g., EfficientNet-B0 instead of B3) to reduce training time if necessary.
Data Quality Issues Low Medium The CATCH dataset has been curated by board-certified pathologists. However, any corrupted or ambiguous slides will be flagged during preprocessing and excluded with documented justification.
Class Imbalance High Medium Oversampling (SMOTE), class-weighted loss functions, and augmentation of minority classes will be employed. Evaluation metrics (macro F1, AUC-ROC) inherently account for imbalanced distributions.
Model Overfitting Medium Medium Regularisation techniques (dropout, weight decay, early stopping) and five-fold cross-validation will mitigate overfitting. Data augmentation further reduces this risk.
Extended Training Duration Medium Medium Week 12 has been designated as a contingency buffer. If training exceeds projections, the number of hyperparameter configurations will be reduced while maintaining core experiments.
Supervisor Unavailability Low Low Regular fortnightly meetings will be scheduled from Week 1. Asynchronous communication via email will ensure continued guidance during periods of limited availability.
