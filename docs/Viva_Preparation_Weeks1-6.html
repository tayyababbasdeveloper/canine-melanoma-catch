1. The big picture (30-second version)

The problem: Skin cancer in dogs is diagnosed by a pathologist looking at a stained tissue slide under a microscope. This is slow, needs an expert, and different experts don't always agree. Melanoma (a pigment-cell cancer) is the focus.

The idea: Train a computer to do two jobs on these slides:

  • 1Segmentation — draw around where the tumour is (find it).
  • 2Classification — say what kind of tumour it is (name it).
If you remember only three things: (1) Stage 1 finds the tumour, Stage 2 names it. (2) The clever model is the Attention U-Net, which learns to "look only where it matters". (3) The code is fully built and tested, but on realistic stand-in (synthetic) images — the real 350-slide dataset is licence-locked and is the next step.

2. The dataset (CATCH), in simple words

CATCH = "CAnine cuTaneous Cancer Histology" — a public research dataset of dog skin-cancer slides (Wilm et al., 2022, hosted on TCIA, DOI 10.7937/TCIA.2M93-FX66).

ThingWhat it means
350 slides (.svs files)Each is a whole-slide image — a single gigantic photo of a tissue sample, often 40,000+ pixels wide.
7 tumour types, 50 slides eachMelanoma, Mast cell tumour, Squamous cell carcinoma, Peripheral nerve sheath tumour, Trichoblastoma, Histiocytoma, Plasmacytoma.
12,424 annotationsDoctors drew polygons around regions (tumour, skin layers, etc.). These become the "correct answers" the model learns from.

The slides are far too big to feed to a network whole, so we cut them into small 256×256 patches (tiles) — this is the unit the models actually see.

3. How the two stages fit together

Whole slide→ cut into 256×256 patches→ Stage 1: U-Net finds tumour (mask)→ keep tumour patches→ Stage 2: ResNet-50 names the type

So segmentation doesn't just locate the cancer — it also selects which patches are worth sending to the classifier. The classifier only ever sees tumour tissue, which makes its job cleaner.

4. Weeks 1–2 — Preparing the images

What I did
  • Quality check — throw out blurry, too-dark, or near-empty slides.
  • Stain normalisation (Macenko) — make every slide use the same colours.
  • Tissue detection — ignore the blank glass, keep only real tissue.
  • Patch extraction at 5×, 10×, 20× zoom levels.
  • Train/validation/test split for fair evaluation.
Why it matters

Garbage in, garbage out. Different labs and scanners produce slides in different shades of pink/purple. If you don't fix this, the model might "cheat" by learning colour instead of biology. Macenko normalisation removes that crutch.

Multiple zoom levels give the model both fine detail (20×) and wider context (5×) — like reading a word and also seeing the whole sentence.

5. Weeks 3–4 — Finding the tumour (U-Net)

What I did

Built and trained a U-Net — the standard network for medical image segmentation. It outputs a mask: for every pixel it answers "tumour or not?".

Two versions were compared:

  • Baseline U-Net — learns everything from scratch.
  • U-Net with a ResNet-34 encoder — reuses a network already trained on millions of everyday photos (transfer learning).
Why a U-Net?

Its "U" shape first shrinks the image to understand what is there, then grows it back to mark where exactly. "Skip connections" carry fine detail across the U so the edges of the tumour stay sharp.

Trained with a BCE + Dice loss, Adam optimiser, and cosine learning-rate schedule — the standard, reliable recipe.

6. Weeks 5–6 — Attention U-Net + naming the tumour

Attention U-Net (Stage 1+)

An upgrade with attention gates: before combining information, the network learns to highlight the tumour region and dim the background — like a highlighter pen that the model controls itself.

Built on the same backbone as the baseline, so any improvement is purely due to attention — a fair, controlled comparison.

ResNet-50 classifier (Stage 2)

Takes the tumour patches and predicts the tumour type. It uses:

  • Transfer learning (starts from ImageNet knowledge).
  • Progressive unfreezing — train the new part first, then gently the rest.
  • Class weighting — pay more attention to rarer tumour types.

7. Key words explained with everyday examples

Whole-slide image (WSI) — one enormous photo of a tissue sample. Like a satellite image of a whole city; you can't look at it all at once.
Patch — a small tile cut from the WSI. Like one street from the satellite image.
Stain normalisation — making all photos use the same colour balance, so brand of camera/lab doesn't matter.
Segmentation — colouring in the exact pixels that are tumour. Output = a black-and-white mask.
Classification — putting an image into one labelled box ("this is melanoma").
Encoder / transfer learning — borrowing a network that already learned to "see" from millions of photos, instead of starting blank. Saves time and data.
Attention gate — a learned spotlight that tells the network where to focus and what to ignore.
Dice score — how well the predicted mask overlaps the true mask. 1.0 = perfect, 0 = no overlap. Like the overlap of two traced shapes.
IoU (Intersection-over-Union) — a stricter cousin of Dice; same idea, always a bit lower.
Hausdorff distance — the worst gap between the predicted and true tumour edge (lower = better).
Class imbalance — some tumour types have far more slides than others; we correct for it so rare types aren't ignored.
Data leakage — accidentally letting the model "peek" at test data during training, which fakes a good score. We prevent it (see §9).

9. Problems I found and fixed

Examiners love this section — it shows you understand why things were wrong, not just that they ran.

Bug 1 · Labels

Fake labels from filenames

The early code guessed the tumour type from the file's colour in its name — biologically meaningless. Fixed: read the real type from the folder/annotation.

Bug 2 · Leakage

Same slide in train & test

Patches from one slide were landing in both training and testing, which fakes a high score. Fixed: split by whole slide, so a slide is only ever in one set (verified zero overlap).

Bug 3 · Transfer learning

Frozen layers weren't really frozen

The "frozen" pretrained layers were quietly drifting during training, undoing the benefit. Fixed: properly lock their statistics (verified: zero drift).

Bonus · Engineering

The Attention U-Net crash

On this PC the Attention U-Net kept crashing. I diagnosed it properly: it wasn't a code bug but a memory (RAM) limit — the bigger model ran out of memory and the system crashed instead of warning. Fix: use a smaller batch size. After that it trained fine (Dice 0.965). This shows debugging skill — finding the real cause instead of guessing.

10. Honest limitations

  • No real data yet. The real CATCH slides are licence-locked and very large (hundreds of GB). Everything so far runs on realistic synthetic images. The code is built to switch to real data with one command — that's the next milestone.
  • Short training. Models ran 2 epochs on CPU for speed; real runs will train much longer.
  • Synthetic = easy. The fake tumours are simpler than real tissue, so the scores are optimistic and will change on real slides.
  • Single split. For the final project, 5-fold cross-validation will give more trustworthy numbers.

Being upfront about these is a strength, not a weakness — it shows you know exactly where the project stands.

11. Likely viva questions & answers ⭐

In one sentence, what is your project?

A two-stage deep-learning pipeline that first finds tumour regions in dog skin-cancer slides (segmentation) and then identifies the tumour type (classification), with a focus on melanoma.

What is the output of the segmentation stage?

A binary mask — a black-and-white image the same size as the patch, where white = tumour pixel and black = everything else. Joined together, these masks show the whole tumour region on the slide.

Why do you need segmentation and classification?

Segmentation answers "where is the cancer?" and also picks out which patches are tumour. Classification answers "what kind of cancer is it?" on just those patches. Together they mimic how a pathologist works: locate, then diagnose.

Why did you choose U-Net?

U-Net is the proven standard for medical image segmentation. Its encoder–decoder ("U") shape captures what is in the image and then restores where precisely, and its skip connections keep tumour edges sharp. It also works well with relatively few images.

What does the attention gate actually do?

Before the network merges detail from the encoder with context from the decoder, the attention gate produces a focus map (values 0–1) that boosts tumour areas and suppresses background. It's a learned spotlight, so the model spends its effort where it matters.

Why is your classification accuracy 100%? Isn't that too good?

Yes, and that's expected. The current data is synthetic and the three classes are drawn in clearly different colours, so they're trivially separable. The 100% only proves the training/evaluation code is correct — not that the model can distinguish real cancers. On real CATCH it becomes a much harder 7-class problem and the score will drop.

Are you using the real dataset?

Not yet — the real CATCH slides need a signed data-use agreement and are hundreds of gigabytes. To keep progressing I validate everything on synthetic slides that exercise every step. The code is already real-data-ready: it reads .svs whole-slide files, parses the real polygon annotations into masks, and knows the 7 official tumour types — switching over is just pointing it at the downloaded slides.

What is data leakage and how did you avoid it?

Leakage is when information from the test set sneaks into training, faking a high score. With slides cut into many patches, the danger is putting patches from the same slide in both training and testing. I split by whole slide, so every patch of a slide stays in one set only. I verified there is zero slide overlap.

How do you handle class imbalance?

Two places. In segmentation, tumour pixels are rare (~10%), so the loss gives tumour pixels more weight (pos_weight) plus a Dice term. In classification, rarer tumour types get higher weight in the loss, and the split keeps class proportions similar across train/val/test.

What's the difference between Dice and IoU?

Both measure overlap between predicted and true masks. Dice counts the overlap twice in its formula, so it's a bit more forgiving; IoU is stricter and always a little lower. Same idea, two standard yardsticks.

Why stain normalisation?

Slides from different labs/scanners come in different shades. Without normalising, the model could "cheat" by learning colour rather than tissue structure, and would fail on slides from a new lab. Macenko normalisation maps every slide onto a common colour appearance.

Why multiple magnifications (5×/10×/20×)?

Different clues live at different zooms: cell-level detail at 20×, tissue-level patterns at 5×. Giving the model several scales is like letting a doctor switch microscope objectives — more context, better decisions.

Why did the ResNet-34 U-Net score worse than the from-scratch baseline?

Only because of very short training (2 epochs on CPU). With a strong tumour-pixel weight it briefly over-predicted and hadn't settled. It's training noise, not a real finding — with full training the pretrained encoder is expected to help, as the literature shows.

What were the main challenges?

(1) No real data, so building a faithful synthetic stand-in and keeping the code truly real-data-ready. (2) Slow CPU training and a memory crash on the Attention U-Net, which I traced to a RAM limit and fixed with a smaller batch size. (3) Catching subtle correctness bugs (label source, data leakage, frozen-layer drift).

What would you do differently / next?

Run on the real CATCH slides, train for full length, add EfficientNet-B3 for comparison, use 5-fold cross-validation and a significance test (McNemar's), and add Grad-CAM heat-maps so a vet can see why the model made each decision.

How is this clinically useful?

It could act as a second opinion — flagging tumour regions and suggesting a type — to speed up reporting and reduce disagreement between pathologists, especially where specialist vets are scarce. It assists, it doesn't replace, the expert.
